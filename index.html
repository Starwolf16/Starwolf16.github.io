Come Join the 2nd Great Awakening

The Second Great Awakening is when the Protestant Religion is going through a revial. We are led by Baptist and Methodist leaders, for the most part, and is a reaction to skepticism, deism, and Rational Chrisitanity. The In the Second Great awakening, we believe in Arminian Theology, which says that every person can be saved through revivals, repentance, and conversion.

Our Leaders

Charles Grandison Finney Lyman Beecher Barton Stone Peter Cartwright
Charles Gandison Finney Converted to christianity in 1821, and since then, he has been one of our most prominent speakers in the Northern States. He is constantly filling the largest speech halls available, and conducts powerful revival meetings all over the easter cities. His revival meeting in Rochester, New York caused 1500 more revivals in towns and villages. Lyman Beecher is one of the preachers of our movement. He started off preaching at a Presbyterian Church, but eventually moved to Calvinism. When he was called to the Hanover Church of Boston, he preached against unitarianism, which was sweeping the area. He went on to teach at the Lane Seminary in Concinnati. There, he taught many ministers to win the West for Protestantism. Barton W. Stone is an important Minister in our midst, and he helped to lead the cane Ridge Revival, which was a several day communion season attened by 20,000 people. When he disagreed with the Kentucky synod, he and 4 other ministers created the Springfield Presbytry, which attracted 15 congregations in Ohio and Kentucky. Peter Cartwright was a Methodist Revivalist who helped start the Second Great Awakening. He personally baptised 12000 converts. Since he was opposed to slavery, he moved from Kentucky to Illinois, and was elected to the lower house of the Illinois Great Assembly in 1828 and 1832. When he was a Methodist Circuit rider, he rode the circuits in Kentucky and Illinois, as well as Tennessee, Indiana, and Ohio.

Synthesis

During the Second Great Awakening, people were focused on religion, and what it could do for them. For example, there were many converts to both the Methodist and Baptist denominations of Christianity. Much of the same happened during the First Great Awakening. Also, the Second Great Awakening was a rebuttal against the rising Skeptism, Deism, and Rational Chrisitanty. These ideas are echoed during the Age of Enlightenment. During the time of the Age of Enlightenment, the First Great Awakening was happening, and one can assume that the First Great Awakening, while not a specific reaction to the ideals of enlightenment, was against it. The people that were mentioned above were able to attract large crowds when they spoke. For example, Barton Stone was able to attract twenty thousand people to one of his sessions, which was over a few days. One of the leaders of the First Great Awakening, George Whitefield, as also able to attract large and emotional crowds to his itinerant sermons.
